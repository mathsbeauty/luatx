1 Sequence and subsequence

  • A sequence of real numbers is a function from the set I of positive real numbers to the set R of real numbers.
    I:12⋯n⋯
    ↓ ↓ ↓ ⋯ ↓ ⋯
    R: s1s2⋯sn⋯

    The above sequence is denoted as {sn}∞n=1. Here sn denote image of n.
  • A subsequence {sni}∞i=1 of {sn}∞n=1is obtained by removing some of the terms of the sequence {sn}∞n=1 without disturbing the order of elements of {sn}∞n=1.
  • Every sequence is subsequence of itself.
    {sn}∞n=1:s1s2s3⋯
    {sni}∞i=1: s1s2s3⋯
    {sni}∞i=1 is a subsequence of {sn}∞n=1. Here ni=i
  • {sn}∞n=1:s1s2s3⋯
    {sni}∞i=1: s1s3s5⋯
    {sni}∞i=1 is a subsequence of {sn}∞n=1. Here ni=2i−1
  • {sn}∞n=1:s1s2s3⋯
    {sni}∞i=1: s2s4s6⋯
    {sni}∞i=1 is a subsequence of {sn}∞n=1. Here ni=2i
  • {sn}∞n=1:s1s2s3⋯
    {sni}∞i=1: s1s4s9⋯
    {sni}∞i=1 is a subsequence of {sn}∞n=1. Here ni=i2

2 Convergence of sequence

  • A sequence {sn}∞n=1 is said to converge to L if for every ϵ>0, there is N∈I such that
    n≥N⇒|sn−L|<ϵ
    In this case we write limn→∞sn=L.
  • limn→∞sn=L⇔∀ϵ>0∃N∈I(n≥N⇒|sn−L|<ϵ)

    limn→∞sn=L⇔∀ϵ>0∃N∈I(n≥N⇒Sn∈(L−ϵ,L+ϵ))

    limn→∞sn=L⇔∀ϵ>0∃N∈I(SN,SN+1,…∈(L−ϵ,L+ϵ))

    limn→∞sn=L⇔∀ϵ>0∃N∈I(L−ϵ,L+ϵ)↑sN,sN+1,sN+2,…

    limn→∞sn=L⇔sn is close to L for sufficiently large n.
  • If {sn}∞n=1 and {tn}∞n=1 are convergent sequences of real numbers then
    limn→∞(sn+tn)=limn→∞sn+limn→∞tn

    limn→∞(sn−tn)=limn→∞sn−limn→∞tn

    limn→∞(sn.tn)=limn→∞sn.limn→∞tn implying that limn→∞smn=(limn→∞sn)m for any positive integer m

    limn→∞(sntn)=limn→∞snlimn→∞tn provided limn→∞tn≠0

3 Monotone sequence

  • A sequence {sn}∞n=1 of real numbers is increasing if
    s1≤s2≤s3≤…
    {sn}∞n=1 is increasing ⇔ (n∈I⇒sn≤sn+1)
    An increasing sequence is also called non-decreasing.
    {sn}∞n=1={n}∞n=1 is increasing as
    1≤2≤3≤…≤n≤n+1≤…
  • A sequence {sn}∞n=1 of real numbers is decreasing if
    s1≥s2≥s3≥…≥
    {sn}∞n=1 is decreasing ⇔ (n∈I⇒sn≥sn+1)
    A decreasing sequence is also called non-increasing.
    {sn}∞n=1={1n}∞n=1 is decreasing as
    11≥12≥13≥…
  • {sn}∞n=1 is monotone if it is increasing or decreasing sequence. Thus {n}∞n=1 and {1n}∞n=1 are monotone sequences. The sequence {(−1)n}∞n=1 is not monotone as it is neither increasing nor decreasing sequence.

4 Bounded sequence

  • A sequence {sn}∞n=1 of real numbers is bounded if there is M>0 such that |sn|≤M for all n∈I.
  • {sn}∞n=1 is bounded ⇔∃M>0(n∈I⇒|sn|≤M)

    {sn}∞n=1 is bounded ⇔∃M>0(n∈I⇒−M≤sn≤M)

    {sn}∞n=1 is bounded ⇔∃M>0(n∈I⇒sn∈[−M,M])

    {sn}∞n=1 is bounded ⇔∃M>0⎛⎜ ⎜ ⎜ ⎜ ⎜⎝[−M,M]↑s1,s2,s3,…⎞⎟ ⎟ ⎟ ⎟ ⎟⎠

    {sn}∞n=1 is bounded ⇔ all terms of {sn}∞n=1 can be enclosed in some closed and bounded interval.
    {sn}∞n=1 is bounded ⇔ all terms of {sn}∞n=1 can be enclosed in some open and bounded interval.
  • The terms of {1n}∞n=1 can be enclosed in interval (0,2). The terms of {(−1)n}∞n=1 can be enclosed in (−2,2).So the sequences {1n}∞n=1 and {(−1)n}∞n=1 are bounded sequences.
  • The terms of {n}∞n=1 and {−n}∞n=1 can’t be enclosed in some bounded intervals. So the sequences {n}∞n=1 and {−n}∞n=1 are unbounded.

5 Cauchy sequence

  • A sequence {sn}∞n=1 is Cauchy if for every ϵ>0, there is N∈I such that
    m,n≥N⇒|sm−sn|<ϵ
  • {sn}∞n=1 is Cauchy ⇔ ∀ϵ>0∃N∈I(m,n≥N⇒|sm−sn|<ϵ)

    {sn}∞n=1 is Cauchy ⇔ ∀ϵ>0∃N∈I(m>n≥N⇒|sm−sn|<ϵ)

    {sn}∞n=1 is Cauchy ⇔ sm and sn are close to each other for sufficiently large m and n.

  • Recall that
    limn→∞sn=L⇔sn is close to L for sufficiently large n.
    The following argument gives a non-rigorous proof of the fact that every Cauchy sequence is convergent.
    {sn}∞n=1 converges to L
    ⇒sm and sn are close to L for sufficiently large m,n
    ⇒sm and sn are close to each other for sufficiently large m,n
    (Analogy: If two persons are close to third person, then these two persons are also close to each other)
    ⇒{sn}∞n=1 is Cauchy

6 Limit superior and inferior

  • Let {sn}∞n=1 be an upper bounded sequence of real numbers. For each n in I, define
    Mn=sup{sn,sn+1…}
    As {sn+1,sn+2,…}⊆{sn,sn+1,…}
    Mn+1≤Mn for all n∈I.
    Thus
    M1≥M2≥M3≥…
    ⇒{Mn}∞n=1 is decreasing.
    So if {Mn}∞n=1 is lower bounded, then {Mn}∞n=1 converges and in this case we define
    limn→∞supsn=limn→∞Mn
    If {Mn}∞n=1 is not lower bounded, then {Mn}∞n=1 diverges to −∞ and in this case we define
    limn→∞supsn=−∞
  • If {sn}∞n=1 is not upper bounded then we define
    limn→∞supsn=∞
  • Let {sn}∞n=1 be a lower bounded sequence of real numbers. For each n in I, define
    mn=inf{sn,sn+1,sn+2,…}
    Since {sn+1,sn+2,…}⊆{sn,sn+1,…}
    mn+1≥mn for all n∈I.
    Thus
    m1≤m2≤m3≤…≤mn+1≤mn≤…
    ⇒{mn}∞n=1 is increasing.
    So if {mn}∞n=1 is upper bounded, then {mn}∞n=1 converges and in this case we define
    limn→∞infsn=limn→∞mn
    If {mn}∞n=1 is not upper bounded, then {mn}∞n=1 diverges to ∞ and in this case we define
    limn→∞infsn=∞
  • If {sn}∞n=1 is not lower bounded then we define
    limn→∞supsn=−∞
  • The number limn→∞supsn gives the idea about how large the terms of {sn}∞n=1 can be when n becomes large and large.
  • The number limn→∞infsn gives the idea about how small the terms of {sn}∞n=1 can be when n becomes large and large.
  • It follows that
    {sn}∞n=1 converges ⇔limn→∞supsn=limn→∞infsn

7 Results

  • Archimedian property of real numbers: For every real number x, there is a positive integer n such that n>x.
    For example if x=0 then n can be taken as 1 or any positive integer.
    If x=10.9 then n can be taken as 11 or any positive integer greater than 10.
    If x=−10.9 then n can be taken as 1 or any positive integer.
    This simple property is useful in proving many of the results such as limn→∞1n=0 and some inclusions.
  • An increasing and upper-bounded sequence of real numbers converges to its supremum.
    s1≤s2≤… and {sn}∞n=1 is upper bounded ⇒ limn→∞sn=sup{sn,sn+1…}
  • An increasing and non upper-bounded sequence of real numbers diverges to infinity.
    s1≤s2≤… and {sn}∞n=1 is not upper bounded ⇒ limn→∞sn=∞
  • A decreasing and lower-bounded sequence of real numbers diverges to its infimum.
    s1≥s2≥… and {sn}∞n=1 is lower bounded ⇒ limn→∞sn=inf{sn,sn+1…}
  • A decreasing and non lower-bounded sequence of real numbers diverges to minus infinity.
    s1≥s2≥… and {sn}∞n=1 is not lower bounded ⇒ limn→∞sn=−∞
  • Every convergent sequence of real numbers is bounded.
    {sn}∞n=1 is Convergent ⇒{sn}∞n=1 is bounded.
  • Every Cauchy sequence of real numbers is bounded.
    {sn}∞n=1 is Cauchy ⇒{sn}∞n=1 is bounded.
  • A sequence of real numbers is Convergent if and only if it is Cauchy.
    {sn}∞n=1 is Convergent ⇔{sn}∞n=1 is Cauchy.
  • {sn}∞n=1 is convergent ⇔limn→∞supsn=limn→∞infsn
